- name: Alpaca (alpaca.markets / Alpaca Markets)
- type: company

## note on scope
- This repo keeps only public-safe company research.
- Private interview/process/self-evaluation notes are kept in a personal knowledge base outside this repo and are intentionally not shown here.

## current hypothesis
- Alpaca is not mainly a retail brokerage app story. It is a developer-first, regulated brokerage-infrastructure company with a direct algorithmic-trading product attached.
- Upside looks real: meaningful scale, strong developer surface area, serious market-structure progress, and visible global ambition.
- Likely cost: high regulatory load, high detail tax, cross-functional coordination, and a work environment where engineering, product, ops, compliance, and brokerage reality constantly collide.

## what I want to know
- whether my target team is building durable systems or living in permanent urgency
- whether the local manager creates focus or routes pressure downward
- how much of the real work is product-building versus regulation-driven exception handling
- whether remote work is genuinely async-friendly or just globally distributed but timezone-heavy
- whether the company’s center of gravity is enterprise brokerage infrastructure, direct trader tooling, or tokenization/crypto expansion

## signals
- evidence:
  - About page: Alpaca says its mission is "opening financial services to everyone on the planet" and frames itself as proprietary technology plus regulatory setup for global investing infrastructure.
  - Broker page: Alpaca markets an end-to-end API platform for stocks, options, fixed income, and crypto, including KYC, onboarding, funding, documents, portfolio rebalancing, fully paid securities lending, local currency trading, tokenization, and 24/5 trading.
  - About page: Alpaca says it powers hundreds of institutions across 40+ countries, has 61,000+ monthly active developers, and started in 2015 as a database and machine-learning company that built the open-source MarketStore database.
  - About page: Alpaca Securities became a FINRA/SEC-registered US broker-dealer in 2018; Alpaca says it was DTCC-approved in 2023, became fully self-clearing in 2024, and added OCC, FICC, and Nasdaq memberships in 2025.
  - Official pages show multiple regulated entities: Alpaca Securities LLC, Alpaca Crypto LLC, AlpacaJapan Co., Ltd., and international entities in the Bahamas.
  - Hiring page: Alpaca says it is "100% remote across 20+ countries" and explicitly emphasizes high-quality/high-impact work, goal orientation, questions from all levels, "own the unknown," and communication that is "radical, honest, and empathy-driven."
  - Official Greenhouse feed showed 63 active roles when checked, spanning Brokerage, Compliance, Crypto, Data Analytics, Design, Engineering, Finance, Info Security, Legal, Marketing, Operations, People, Product, Sales, Success, and Tech Operations.
  - A live engineering posting for Senior Frontend Engineer - Trading API describes Alpaca as a US-headquartered self-clearing broker-dealer and brokerage infrastructure company, asks for React/TypeScript plus mobile and some systems-language depth, and values REST/WebSockets/SQL, remote experience, financial-markets understanding, and attention to detail.
  - The same job posting says Alpaca raised a $150M Series D at a $1.15B valuation in January 2026 and now has over $320M total investment.
  - Status page: Alpaca publicly exposes uptime and incidents, currently shows "All Systems Operational," and also posts a recurring monthly maintenance window with possible downtime up to 2.5 hours.
  - GitHub org: Alpaca has 88 public repositories and about 1.4k followers, with visible SDK/community adoption (for example alpaca-py at about 1.3k stars and the official alpaca-mcp-server at about 670 stars when checked).
  - Official disclosures page includes broker-dealer/compliance materials such as fee schedules, Rule 606 reports, and payment-for-order-flow disclosures, which is evidence that the monetization and routing stack is more complex than a simple "free trading" story.
- interpretation:
  - This looks much closer to brokerage infrastructure / brokerage-as-a-service than to a consumer-first brokerage like Robinhood.
  - The real moat is probably not just API ergonomics. It is control of the stack: developer surface area + self-clearing + memberships + regulatory footprint + global partner distribution.
  - Work here likely has a strong reality layer. Even product and engineering roles seem hard to separate from brokerage operations, settlement, compliance, incident management, and market-structure constraints.
  - The company appears ambitious and expansionary, especially around self-clearing, international distribution, and tokenization/on-chain rails. That likely creates genuine upside but also organizational churn and priority motion.
  - Remote does not look like a lifestyle perk first. It looks like an operating model that assumes autonomy, communication discipline, and comfort with ambiguity.
  - The hiring language is attractive, but it also signals a standards-heavy environment: details matter, ownership matters, and people are expected to operate without being spoon-fed.
- uncertainty:
  - public materials do not reveal whether my specific manager/team is protective, chaotic, political, or unusually healthy
  - unclear how much day-to-day work is durable systems work versus exception handling driven by regulation, partners, or incidents
  - unclear whether the most important internal customer is enterprise partners, active traders, compliance/risk, or executive expansion bets
  - unclear how much cross-timezone load and meeting tax remote employees actually absorb
  - unclear whether recent scale and funding have made the org more coherent or just more complex

## company model read
- Core business: regulated brokerage infrastructure with APIs for partners, plus direct trading products for active traders/algo users.
- Likely revenue stack: enterprise infrastructure fees, market-data subscriptions, premium trader plans, margin/cash/lending economics, brokerage routing economics, and crypto-related revenue.
- Why this matters: the company probably cannot be understood as "just another API startup." Regulation, custody, clearing, partner operations, and market plumbing are part of the product.

## workplace read
- healthy signal:
  - company has real product surface, real infra, real customers, real disclosures, and visible developer/community artifacts
  - public status page and disclosures suggest operational seriousness rather than pure marketing theater
  - open-source and SDK presence suggest the developer story is not fake
- mixed signal:
  - public metrics vary by page/date: 7M+ vs 9M+ brokerage accounts, 200+ vs 300+ partner organizations, 20+ vs 25 countries, 230+ vs 250+ employees
  - this may be normal page staleness, but it also means I should treat headline numbers carefully
- concerning signal:
  - self-clearing, crypto, tokenization, and global expansion all increase operational and regulatory complexity
  - a company can be exciting and still impose constant coordination tax, incident stress, and compliance pressure

## likely fit read
- stronger fit if I want: serious systems, developer-facing products, market-infrastructure depth, global scope, and a place where software meets real institutions
- weaker fit if I want: low-regulation product work, cleanly bounded engineering scope, or a mostly local/simple operating environment

## interview angles to test
- Ask what part of the company actually drives roadmap priority right now: enterprise partners, trader product, compliance/risk, or expansion.
- Ask for a recent example where regulation or operations forced a product/engineering tradeoff.
- Ask how incidents, maintenance windows, and cross-team escalations are handled in practice.
- Ask what distinguishes a strong manager at Alpaca from a weak one.
- Ask what has become easier versus harder after self-clearing and recent scale/funding.
- Ask which work is genuinely strategic and which work is recurring brokerage plumbing that never goes away.

## decision
- current recommendation: continue cautiously
- reason: the company still looks technically meaningful and aligned with an infrastructure/trust-oriented reading, but the real manager/team operating quality is still the key unknown
- what would change my mind:
  - strong evidence that the target manager protects focus and tells the truth about tradeoffs
  - concrete examples of durable system-building rather than permanent reactive execution
  - signs that remote collaboration is intentional rather than always-on
